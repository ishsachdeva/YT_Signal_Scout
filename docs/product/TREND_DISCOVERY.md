# Trend Discovery

## Purpose

Define trends, their lifecycle, and responsible future research directions.

## Definition and role

A Trend is a time-dependent directional change in audience attention, creator supply, content
format, behaviour, or observable performance. It adds timing context to topics and niches. Popularity
is a level; a trend is change. A niche may persist without trending, and a trend may cross multiple
niches without creating an accessible opportunity.

## Lifecycle

Future Product language may distinguish emerging, accelerating, established, decelerating, and
retired states, but these states require research definitions before use. Lifecycle assessment must
state observation windows, comparison context, evidence coverage, and update time. A trend may recur,
transform, or prove to be noise; history should be retained rather than relabelled retroactively.

## Evidence

Possible evidence includes multi-channel upload activity, topic prevalence, recent performance,
search or platform data available through authorized sources, cross-region propagation, content
pattern adoption, persistence across observation windows, and counterevidence. Cross-channel and
longitudinal support is essential. Trend evidence alone does not establish unmet demand,
repeatability, production feasibility, or monetization.

## False positives

Common false positives include one viral upload, a creator-specific event, paid promotion, external
news shocks, seasonal recurrence treated as permanent growth, platform distribution tests, duplicate
or coordinated content, metric corrections, sparse data, and supply growth mistaken for audience
demand. Explanations should disclose plausible alternatives.

## Scope and limitations

This document defines no velocity metric, forecast, trend score, alert threshold, or prediction. The
platform must not imply that emerging attention will continue or that users can reproduce it.

## Future considerations

Research observation cadence, seasonal baselines, topic identity drift, geographic propagation,
source coverage, persistence, change-point validation, coordinated activity, and human-readable
lifecycle communication before implementation.
